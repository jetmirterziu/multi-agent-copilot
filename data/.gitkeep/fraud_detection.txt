FRAUD DETECTION AND PREVENTION GUIDELINES
Insurance Special Investigations Unit (SIU)
Version 3.2 - Updated November 2023

CHAPTER 1: FRAUD OVERVIEW

1.1 TYPES OF INSURANCE FRAUD
Hard Fraud: Deliberately faking or causing an incident to collect insurance money
- Staged accidents
- Arson for profit
- Fake theft claims
- Phantom injuries

Soft Fraud: Exaggerating legitimate claims
- Inflating damage amounts
- Adding unrelated damage to claim
- Falsifying dates of loss
- Overstating value of stolen items

1.2 FRAUD STATISTICS
- Estimated 10% of all property-casualty claims involve fraud
- Annual cost to industry: $40 billion
- Average fraudulent claim amount: $12,500
- Detection rate: Only 25% of fraud is identified

CHAPTER 2: RED FLAGS AND INDICATORS

2.1 AUTO INSURANCE FRAUD INDICATORS
Accident-related:
- Police report filed several days after accident
- Claimant has history of similar accidents
- Damage inconsistent with accident description
- No independent witnesses
- Accident occurred in isolated location
- All occupants claim soft tissue injuries
- Passengers unknown to driver or recently acquainted

Vehicle-related:
- Recent policy purchase or coverage increase
- Vehicle has existing damage
- Title issues or salvage history
- Odometer reading suspicious
- Recent change of address to high-crime area

2.2 HOMEOWNERS FRAUD INDICATORS
Property damage:
- Claim filed immediately after policy inception
- Prior similar claims with other insurers
- Loss occurred during financial hardship
- Multiple claims for same type of damage
- Damage appears intentional
- No emergency repairs attempted

Theft claims:
- No forced entry evidence
- High-value items with no receipts
- Claim includes many recently purchased items
- Reluctance to provide receipts or proof of ownership
- Items claimed exceed policy limits
- No police report filed

Fire/arson indicators:
- Fire origin consistent with accelerant use
- Multiple fire origins detected
- Property heavily insured shortly before fire
- Valuable items removed before fire
- Fire occurred when occupants conveniently absent
- Recent business or financial troubles

2.3 INJURY FRAUD INDICATORS
- Treatment primarily with chiropractor or attorney-affiliated provider
- Injury type difficult to verify medically (soft tissue)
- Treatment frequency excessive for injury type
- Late onset of treatment (weeks after incident)
- Pre-existing condition undisclosed
- Medical bills significantly exceed typical costs
- Attorney involvement before medical treatment

CHAPTER 3: INVESTIGATION PROTOCOLS

3.1 PRELIMINARY ASSESSMENT
When red flags present, adjuster must:
- Document all suspicious indicators
- Search internal database for prior claims
- Review social media profiles (public information only)
- Check for prior fraud reports
- Escalate to SIU if 3 or more red flags present

3.2 SIU REFERRAL CRITERIA
Mandatory referral if:
- Suspected staged accident
- Arson indicators present
- Claimed theft exceeds $25,000
- Conflicting statements from insured
- Provider billing inconsistencies
- Medical treatment appears unnecessary

3.3 INVESTIGATION TECHNIQUES
Surveillance:
- Video surveillance of claimant (public areas only)
- Activity checks (social media, employment records)
- Witness interviews
- Scene investigation

Database searches:
- Index Bureau (ISO) claims history
- National Insurance Crime Bureau (NICB) database
- Court records search
- Property records
- Business records

Expert examination:
- Independent medical examination
- Fire origin and cause investigation
- Vehicle damage analysis
- Forensic document examination

3.4 INVESTIGATION TIMELINE
- Initial review: 48 hours of referral
- Full investigation: Complete within 30 days
- Final report: Within 45 days
- Law enforcement referral: Within 60 days if evidence warrants

CHAPTER 4: EVIDENCE COLLECTION

4.1 ADMISSIBLE EVIDENCE
Document thoroughly:
- Date, time, location of all activities
- Names and contact info of witnesses
- Photographs and videos with metadata
- Signed statements
- Chain of custody for physical evidence

4.2 INTERVIEW BEST PRACTICES
- Conduct early before stories become rehearsed
- Record with consent (follow state laws)
- Ask open-ended questions
- Note inconsistencies
- Obtain signed statement

Questions to ask:
- Exact sequence of events
- Who was present
- Time of incident
- Weather conditions
- Previous damage or issues
- Financial situation
- Recent life changes

4.3 DOCUMENTATION REQUIREMENTS
All investigations must include:
- Detailed investigator notes
- Timeline of events
- Evidence log
- Witness statements
- Photographs and videos
- Expert reports
- Database search results

CHAPTER 5: CLAIM HANDLING FOR SUSPECTED FRAUD

5.1 INVESTIGATION DURING CLAIM PROCESSING
- Continue normal claim investigation
- Do not accuse claimant of fraud
- Request additional documentation
- Delay payment pending investigation
- Maintain detailed file notes

5.2 RESERVATION OF RIGHTS
Send reservation of rights letter if:
- Policy coverage question exists
- Fraud suspected but not confirmed
- Cooperation clause violation

Letter must specify:
- Specific policy provisions in question
- Additional information needed
- Investigation timeline
- Rights reserved by insurer

5.3 DENIAL PROCEDURES
If fraud confirmed:
- Deny claim in writing
- State specific reasons
- Reference policy fraud provisions
- Return all premiums if material misrepresentation
- Document all evidence thoroughly

CHAPTER 6: LEGAL ACTIONS

6.1 REPORTING TO AUTHORITIES
Report to law enforcement when:
- Evidence of criminal activity
- Organized fraud ring suspected
- Fraud amount exceeds $10,000
- Violence involved
- Licensed professional involved (attorney, doctor)

6.2 REPORTING TO INSURANCE FRAUD BUREAU
Mandatory reporting to state fraud bureau:
- All suspected fraud cases
- Report within 30 days of suspicion
- Include detailed evidence summary

6.3 CIVIL ACTIONS
Pursue civil recovery if:
- Fraud confirmed through investigation
- Criminal conviction obtained
- Recovery amount exceeds legal costs
- Deterrent value to prevent future fraud

CHAPTER 7: FRAUD PREVENTION

7.1 UNDERWRITING CONTROLS
- Verify prior insurance history
- Check claims databases
- Require property inspections
- Review financial stability
- Investigate suspicious application patterns

7.2 CLAIMS CONTROLS
- Random quality audits
- Special investigations unit review
- Data analytics for pattern detection
- Training for claims adjusters
- Fraud awareness programs

7.3 TECHNOLOGY SOLUTIONS
Implement:
- Predictive analytics models
- Social network analysis
- Text mining of claim notes
- Image analysis for damage consistency
- Automated red flag detection

CHAPTER 8: PROVIDER FRAUD

8.1 MEDICAL PROVIDER FRAUD INDICATORS
- Billing for services not rendered
- Upcoding (billing for more expensive service)
- Unbundling (separate billing for procedures normally bundled)
- Excessive or unnecessary treatment
- Kickbacks for patient referrals
- Same diagnosis code for all patients

8.2 AUTO REPAIR FRAUD INDICATORS
- Inflated repair estimates
- Charging for new parts but using aftermarket/used
- Billing for repairs not performed
- Colluding with claimant on betterment
- Recommending unnecessary repairs

8.3 PROVIDER MONITORING
- Review billing patterns
- Compare to industry standards
- Site visits to facilities
- Patient outcome tracking
- Audit of medical records vs. billing

8.4 NETWORK MANAGEMENT
Action for fraudulent providers:
- Remove from preferred provider network
- Report to licensing board
- Refer to law enforcement
- Pursue civil recovery
- Share information with other insurers

CHAPTER 9: ORGANIZED FRAUD RINGS

9.1 CHARACTERISTICS
- Multiple related claims
- Same participants across claims
- Similar incident patterns
- Common providers or legal representation
- Geographically clustered

9.2 INVESTIGATION APPROACH
- Map relationships between participants
- Cross-reference all claims
- Coordinate with other insurers
- Involve law enforcement early
- Use RICO statutes if applicable

CHAPTER 10: TRAINING AND AWARENESS

10.1 EMPLOYEE TRAINING
All claims staff must complete:
- Annual fraud awareness training
- Red flag identification course
- Investigation documentation procedures
- Legal and ethical considerations

10.2 INDUSTRY COLLABORATION
Participate in:
- National Insurance Crime Bureau programs
- State fraud bureau coalitions
- Industry working groups
- Information sharing networks
